Sequences and Series


This module will provide a brief understanding of sequences and series and an insight into their daily-life applications.

Geometric-Progression:

A sequence is called a geometric progression if the ratio of any term to its previous term is a constant, provided that each term is non-zero.
A geometric progression is of the form:

a, ar, ar2, ar3, ar4 where a is the first term and r is the common ratio

we can clearly see that: ar⁄a = ar2⁄ar = ar3⁄ar2 = r (common-ratio)

Geometric-progresssion ~ Daily life examples:-

The size of a population in exponential growth, e.g. bacteria in a Petri dish (or in your leftovers)

The intensity of radioactivity after n years of a given radioactive material (with application to determining the age of mommies!).

How is the age of mummies calculated?


Living things breathe in Carbon-14(the most abundant isotope of Carbon) and incorporate it into their bodies.
As long as they continue breathing, the amount of C-14 is stable (same amount out as in), but once they die they stop taking in new C-14.
All radioactive elements have a 'half-life': an amount of time that it takes for half of the radioactive atoms(making it a GP with constant ratio to be 1⁄2) to undergo nuclear decay.
For C-14, the half time is 5730 years. That means that after one half-life, 5730 years, half the C-14 will have decayed, after two half-lives, 11460 years, half of what remains will have decayed, so there will be one-fourth of the original amount. And so on.
The best-known are the Egyptian mummies that are about 5500 years old, so a little less than one half-life.
Counting the amount of C-14 decays occurring provides a measure of how much remains, and allows the age of the formerly living objects( such as mummies) to be calculated.
