Travel and Motion-Parallax


When we were in primary school, We liked travelling a lot. Every time we were on the train what we did was listening to music and watching landscape outside. During the traveling, the outside view was usually countryside where most of field was undeveloped or farming environment with small houses. There were usually mountains in the distance and sometimes domestic animals such as cows and horses. It was a matter of fascination for all of us that as the train picked up speed, objects moved and disappeared faster. Also, the objects that were closer to us vanished from our sight more rapidly than the ones which were at a distance from us such that we would lean through the window and look outside just as to see where the poll went that caught our sight for a brief instance of time. At the same time, most of us liked how the sun and moon "liked" following us and grant us their company throughout the ride. However, We did not know that what made us feel unique and special was common to everybody and indeed a scientific phenomenon. Yes it is motion-parallax.

Motion parallax is one of many monocular depth cues that help people to determine distance between objects. It provides perceptual cues especially about difference in distance and motion. Images closer to the observer appear to move faster across the visual field than images farther away. It means that objects that are moving faster are closer to the observer but there is more. It is similar to the idea of relative height, which is also one of the monocular depth cues. When objects are below the horizon, objects higher in the visual field appear to be farther away and when objects are above the horizon, objects lower in the visual field appear to be farther away. The reason that motion parallax is similar to relative height is because when there are no obstacles that block further view point, objects that are above the horizon moves in opposite direction than are below the horizon. Not only that, when objects are above the horizon objects move to same direction of the observer such as the sun, moon, distant mountains and objects lower in the visual field, which is closer to the observer, move faster than those in higher visual field in the opposite direction.
